## How The System Works

Explain your design in plain language.
I understand how recommendations use certain attributes to narrow down and evaluate what a unique user is interested in and show them either similar content or new content that feels familiar. With a platform like Youtube, it looks through all the songs/music videos that I've seen and use an algorithm to create a mix playlist for me to checkout with old and new songs/music videos alike based on my preferences in genre, mood, artist, and energy. My version will mainly focus on genre, mood, and energy as they all hold the most weight and equal weight in some cases. There's also the fact that an artist can make many songs in different moods and energies, so having a bit more of a broad selection will be helpful for my recommender. Each Song object will pull data directly from the songs.csv file. While the file contains many attributes, my recommender will focus on these specific ones: genre(string), mood(string), energy(float), and tempo(integer). The UserProfile represents the person looking for music. Instead of just "liking" a song, the profile stores specific preferences: preferred genres, target mood, and energy level. For every song in your catalog, the system calculates a score based on how well it matches the UserProfile. If the song's genre is in the user's "Preferred Genres" list, its a genre match worth 50 points. If the song's mood exactly matches the user's "Target Mood", that a mood match worth 50 points. Finally, it can be calculated for how "close" the song's energy is to the user's preferred energy proximity, worth 20 points. The energy score formula works as follows: EnergyScore = 20 X (1 - |UserEnergy - SongEnergy|). Once every song has a total score, the recommender will sort the list from highest score to lowest score, select the top 3 or 5 songs to show the user, and if two songs have the same score, the one with a Tempo closer to the average (e.g., 100 BPM) is chosen first.

The system will calculate a Total Compatibility Score for every song in the catalog out of a possible 150 points. Categorical Foundations: These rules determine the "core" of the recommendation. They use the favorite_genres and favorite_moods lists from an updated profile. If the song's genre is in a favorites list, it earns 50 points. If the song's mood is in a favorites list, it earns 40 points. Proximity Scoring: For numerical features, "all-or-nothing" matching won't be used. Instead, a proximity formula is used to see how close the song is to the target "vibe". The Proximity Formula: Score = Weight X (1 - |TargetValue - SongValue|). This scoring logic assigns 90 out of 150 total points (roughly 60%) to Genre and Mood matches alone. This creates a system that "pigeonholes" users into specific labels. A song that perfectly matches the target energy, valence, and acousticness but belongs to a different genre will almost always be ranked lower than a song in a favorite genre that has a completely wrong "vibe". Because the system relies on exact string matching for genres like "pop," "indie pop," and "indie rock," it lacks a sense of musical similarity. The algorithm treats "pop" and "indie pop" as being as different as "pop" and "metal". Recommendations may be missed simply because the CSV labels are more specific than a user profile targets.

## 9. Personal Reflection

A few sentences about what you learned:

- What surprised you about how your system behaved
- How did building this change how you think about real music recommenders
- Where do you think human judgment still matters, even if the model seems "smart"

> Building this system was an exercise in turning the subjective "magic" of a music vibe into a concrete mathematical formula. It revealed that recommender systems are essentially just a series of "knobs" or weights that decide which parts of a song's identity—like its label or its raw energy—matter most for the listener. For someone using music to fuel a specific creative process, like writing a new short story, seeing how these variables interact is eye-opening. The most interesting discovery was the "Sticker Bias"—how strongly the system favors a genre label over the actual sound of the music. I found that the system would repeatedly suggest a high-energy Pop track for a "Happy" user even if that song was way too intense, simply because the +50 point "Pop" sticker was too powerful for the energy proximity math to overcome. This creates a situation where the algorithm is more loyal to the "genre box" than to the user's actual needs. Building this changed how I view apps like YouTube or Spotify; I no longer see them as "knowing" my taste, but rather as calculating my proximity to specific clusters of data. It's made me realize that if my own writing flow feels stagnant, it might be because a real-world algorithm has me stuck in a "filter bubble" based on high-weight weights for my past habits. Human judgment still matters because a computer can calculate that two songs are 98% similar, but it can't feel whether that 2% difference is what actually makes a song inspiring.
